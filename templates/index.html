Word Equations

How It Works

Words are represented as points in a 50-dimensional space where similar meanings cluster together. When you subtract one word from another, you create a concept vector that captures the relationship between them. For example, woman - man creates a vector pointing in the "gender direction" from masculine to feminine. When you add this concept vector to a new word like king, you're saying "apply the same transformation" — giving you queen.

What Works Best

These embeddings are trained on Wikipedia and news text, so they excel at relationships that appear frequently in writing:

  • Geography: paris - france + italy → rome, tokyo - japan + china → beijing
  • Languages: french - france + spain → spanish, english - england + germany → german
  • Gender: actress - actor + waiter → waitress, prince - princess + queen → king
  • Verb Tenses: walking - walk + run → running, went - go + do → did
  • Professions: guitarist - guitar + piano → pianist, doctor - hospital + school → teacher
  • Comparatives: better - good + bad → worse, fastest - fast + slow → slowest

Advanced: Scalar Multiplication

You can multiply concept vectors by numbers to control their strength. Use parentheses to group operations: 0.8 * (woman - man) + king applies 80% of the gender transformation. Try 2 * (paris - france) + italy to amplify the capital relationship, or 0.3 * king + 0.7 * queen to blend between concepts.
